Introduction

Panel to right of exhibition entrance:

South Africa the art of a nation

Some of the world's oldest artworks were created in southern Africa 100,000 years ago. The present-day nation of South Africa has a powerful contemporary art scene, one that draws inspiration from this deep past, as well as more recent events, to comment on the present.

This exhibition juxtaposes historic and contemporary artworks to tell a history of South Africa from the earliest artistic acts to the most recent. It highlights the many peoples that have contributed to making the art of the nation of South Africa.

Panel on left-hand wall:
Rock art is one of South Africa's oldest artistic traditions.

Ancient art, new media

Rock art was first made by the ancestors of San|Bushmen and Khoekhoen, South Africa's first peoples, at least 30,000 years ago.

The tradition did not survive the impact of European colonisation in the late 1800s, but rock art is now an important part of South Africa's post-apartheid national identity. The central figures in the South African coat of arms are from the Linton Panel, the most famous example of rock art in South Africa, and the national motto is written in /Xam, a now extinct San|Bushman language:

KE E : /XARRA //KE – Diverse People Unite

Although rock art is no longer made, descendants of San|Bushmen and Khoekhoen continue to make artworks based on their historical traditions.

Panel image caption:

Naming peoples

The joining of the names 'San|Bushmen', which refers to men and women, reflects the political complexities of collective naming, where both 'San' and 'Bushman' are considered derogatory by some but have been positively adopted by others. Lines such as / and ! before certain words represent sounds not present in the Latin alphabet.

From origins to early art

In South Africa the development of artistic activity has left a visible trace of our evolution, establishing it as one of the places where our ancestors first became fully human.

The story begins around three million years ago, with objects that were collected and valued for their appearance, continuing in the deliberate shaping of objects to make them aesthetically pleasing, forerunners of true artistic traditions that began 100,000 years ago.

From at least 77,000 years ago, people began to decorate objects and their own bodies and, eventually, to produce two-dimensional paintings and engravings of humans and animals.

This archaeological evidence identifies South Africa as one of the cradles of humankind, and is an important part of the country's national identity

A cradle of humankind

The deep artistic past is important in contemporary South Africa because it identifies the country as one of the locations where modern human behaviour began. Archaeological evidence also shows it as one of the places where the modern human body developed.

This means that we are all descended from Africa, both in terms of our bodily structure and in the way that we act, think and feel. The findings are an important challenge to Eurocentric interpretations of world history.

Some contemporary artists have found inspiration in the celebration of southern Africa as one of the places where modern humans evolved.

First sculptures

Around 1,500 years ago, Bantu-speaking people migrated into southern Africa. Today the descendants of those migrants make up the majority of the South African population, including the Zulu, Xhosa, Swazi, South Sotho, North Sotho, Ndebele, Tsonga, Tswana and Venda peoples.

The new arrivals created permanent settlements, grew crops and herded cattle, sheep and goats, and brought with them new technologies, such as metalworking. They also took part in an international trade network that stretched across the Indian Ocean. Their wealth led to the creation of the first kingdom in southern Africa.

These societies used sculptures to assert their values, for instance during ceremonies that marked rites of passage. The sculptures are part of the evidence of complex societies in South Africa before the arrival of Europeans.

Terra nullius – the myth of an 'empty land'

From 1948 the South African government introduced laws that enforced a system of racial segregation known as apartheid, meaning 'separateness'. An official version of history was promoted during this period to justify white rule, which stated that South Africa had been an 'empty land' before European settlement in 1652.

In fact South African art shows that people existed in South Africa long before European settlement. Although many of the sculptures in this section were known to exist, they were not included in official histories promoted by the apartheid state.

The contemporary works here express a need to challenge the false ideas sanctioned by apartheid rule.
